The Woman Who Declared Independence

How Elizabeth Cady Stanton sparked a movement that changed the course of American history
By Shreyas Pokala  |  Reform Era, 1820–1860
Thesis
Elizabeth Cady Stanton was highly effective in challenging the legal and social inequalities facing women in nineteenth century America; through the Seneca Falls Convention, the Declaration of Sentiments, and the founding of national suffrage organizations, she transformed personal outrage into a powerful national movement that permanently reshaped American law and society.

Who Was Elizabeth Cady Stanton?

Elizabeth Cady Stanton (1815–1902) was one of the most influential leaders of the American women’s rights movement. Born in Johnstown, New York, she grew up witnessing firsthand how the law treated women as second-class citizens — an injustice that would fuel her lifelong crusade for equality.

A gifted writer, orator, and organizer, Stanton co-organized the landmark Seneca Falls Convention in 1848 and authored the Declaration of Sentiments, a revolutionary document demanding equal rights for women in property, education, marriage, and the ballot. Alongside Susan B. Anthony, she co-founded the National Woman Suffrage Association and spent over fifty years fighting for a constitutional amendment guaranteeing women the right to vote.

Though she did not live to see it, the 19th Amendment — ratified in 1920, eighteen years after her death — was the direct result of the movement she helped build. Her vision reshaped American law and society forever.

Stage I

Identify an Issue

What was going on in America — and why did Stanton act?

In early 19th-century America, women had almost no legal identity. Marriage stripped them of their property, wages, and custody rights. Voting was out of the question. Even reform movements fighting for freedom excluded them.

No Legal Rights

Property, wages & children

An unmarried woman could own property, keep wages, and retain custody of children. Once married, all of these rights vanished. She became legally invisible.

No Political Voice

Voting & representation

tap to learn more
Women could not vote in any election — local, state, or federal. They were excluded from political conventions and had no way to influence the laws governing their lives.

No Escape

Marriage & divorce traps

tap to learn more
Divorce laws heavily favored men. Women in abusive marriages had almost no legal way out. Separation without divorce left them with no property, no income, and no freedom.

“Men wrote the marriage code — and it is unjust to women.”

— E.C. Stanton, Address on Divorce Bill, 1861 (Library of Congress)

The fight for women’s suffrage began within the broader reform movements of the mid-1800s. Leaders disagreed on methods — federal vs. state, petitions vs. protests — but Stanton was determined to act.

Early Life

Family, education & influences

tap to learn more
Born in Johnstown, NY — 8th of 11 children (only 6 survived). Her father was a prominent attorney and congressman. She studied math, Greek, and Latin at Johnstown Academy — rare for women at the time. Graduated from Troy Female Seminary in 1832.

What Radicalized Her

From observer to activist

tap to learn more
Her father showed her how law treated women unequally. In 1840, she married abolitionist Henry Stanton — and removed “obey” from her vows. That same year, women were refused recognition at the World Anti-Slavery Convention in London. This hypocrisy ignited her fight.

Stage III

Develop an Action Plan

What did Stanton set out to do — and how?

“We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men and women are created equal.”

— Declaration of Sentiments, 1848 (Mountjoy and McNeese 42)

Her Goals

  • Women’s right to vote in all elections
  • Married women’s right to own property & wages
  • Women’s right to retain custody of children
  • Reform of marriage and divorce law
  • Equal access to education
  • A federal constitutional amendment for equality

Stanton’s vision went far beyond the ballot. She wanted total legal equality — in property, education, marriage, and politics.

Her Strategies

  • Conventions — large public gatherings to build momentum
  • Writing & Press — newspapers and pamphlets to spread ideas
  • Petitioning Congress — formal lobbying for legislation
  • Alliances — coalitions with abolitionists and reformers
  • Legislative testimony — appearing before committees on specific bills
  • State campaigns — building grassroots pressure for federal action

Target: Lawmakers

tap to learn more
Stanton petitioned Congress directly and testified before state legislatures. She pushed for constitutional amendments and specific reform bills.

Target: The Public

tap to learn more
Through The Revolution newspaper and national speaking tours, she reached thousands of ordinary Americans — making the case that women’s rights were human rights.

Target: Women

tap to learn more
Conventions, clubs, and reform societies organized women directly — giving them a voice, a community, and a path to political action for the first time.
Stage IV

Obstacles Faced

The forces that fought against Stanton and the movement
§

Legal Barriers

Under coverture, married women had no legal identity separate from their husbands. They could not own property, sign contracts, or keep their own wages. Changing these laws meant convincing the very men who benefited from keeping them in place.

¶

Social Opposition

Society viewed women who spoke publicly as improper and unfeminine. Newspapers mocked the Seneca Falls Convention. Ministers cited scripture to argue women belonged in the home. Even many women opposed the movement, fearing social consequences.

†

The 14th Amendment Setback

The 14th Amendment (1868) introduced the word “male” into the Constitution for the first time. Former allies told women to wait their turn, calling it “the Negro’s hour.” This split the reform coalition and set back women’s suffrage by decades.

÷

Movement Division

The NWSA/AWSA split (1869–1890) divided the movement for over 20 years. Stanton’s NWSA pursued a federal amendment while AWSA focused on state campaigns. The rivalry drained resources and slowed progress until their merger in 1890.

♥

Personal Sacrifices

Stanton raised seven children while leading a national movement. Her husband Henry’s mental health struggles added personal strain. She was often confined to domestic duties for years at a time, relying on correspondence and writing to stay active in the cause.

&

Racial Exclusion

The movement struggled with racial exclusion. Black women activists like Sojourner Truth and Mary Church Terrell contributed enormously but remained marginalized. Stanton herself made controversial statements that prioritized white women’s suffrage, weakening potential alliances.

“We are sowing winter wheat, which the coming spring will see sprout and which other hands than ours will reap and enjoy.”

— Elizabeth Cady Stanton, reflecting on the slow pace of reform

Stage VI

Reflect

How effective was Stanton at bringing about change in 19th-century society?

Short-Term Wins

What she achieved in her lifetime

tap to learn more
1848 NY Property Act — married women could own property for the first time.

Seneca Falls — placed women’s rights permanently on the national agenda.

NWSA → NAWSA — built the organizational infrastructure that carried the movement for 50+ years.

Yet the 14th & 15th Amendments excluded women. She died in 1902 — 18 years before women won the vote.

Long-Term Legacy

How her work changed America forever

tap to learn more
The 19th Amendment (1920) — the direct result of the movement she started.

The Declaration of Sentiments remains one of the most important documents in American history.

Her vision — property rights, divorce reform, educational equality — anticipated every major women’s rights advance of the 20th century.

What Could She Have Done Differently?

tap to learn more
The movement struggled with racial exclusion. Black women activists like Mary Church Terrell spoke out but remained marginalized. A broader, more inclusive coalition could have achieved change sooner.

The NWSA/AWSA split divided the movement for over 20 years. Earlier reunification might have accelerated federal action.

Her Work Lives On Today

Modern connections

tap to learn more
The strategies she pioneered — conventions, media campaigns, legislative lobbying, coalitions — are the exact tools used by movements like the Women’s March, #MeToo, and advocates for the Equal Rights Amendment today.

Her insistence on structural, legal change over individual accommodation echoes in every modern civil rights movement.
